## World Overview

### The Planet
**Name:** Aur

The Serev named their world Aur — light, warmth, the word for what sustains you. The world no longer sustains anything. The name is a record of what it was.

The player arrives on a world that was, once, alive. Not with human life — with something older, stranger, and now entirely gone. The ruins of a vast alien civilization cover Aur's surface: crumbling megastructures, dormant machines half-subsumed by geological activity, and the unmistakable impression of a species that built at a scale that implied permanence. They were wrong.

Aur is not dead — but it is dying. The ecology that once sustained the Serev has been disrupted. The atmosphere is thin in places. The geological cycles have slowed. Whatever balanced Aur once no longer functions. The planet is in slow collapse.

At the heart of the ruins, and distributed in nodes across Aur's most extreme biomes, is evidence of a singular project: the **Naer-Reth** — a planetary-scale machine the Serev called "The Chrysalis." Rel translates: *"A container for transformation. The old form dissolves. A new form emerges."* It was never fully activated. The Serev did not survive to see it run.

The player does not yet know that the Naer-Reth is what destroyed them.

### The Alien Civilization
**Name:** The Serev

What is known about them:
- They were spacefaring, or at minimum capable of planetary-scale engineering
- They built the Naer-Reth to address an ecological crisis of their own making
- They began activation. Something went wrong. They are gone.
- Their language is partially decodeable through Rel's translations
- Their architecture is characterized by [TBD — pending art direction; visually distinct from human design]

What is unknown (and may never be fully known):
- Their appearance, biology, culture, and social structure
- The exact sequence of events during the collapse
- Whether any of them escaped

The Serev are known to the player through **ruins, recovered data fragments, and Rel's fragmented recollections**. They are never seen. They are felt.

### What the Player Knows at the Start
- They are a human researcher, sent ahead as part of an interstellar research expedition to study the Serev before it was too late
- They went into cryo en route
- They arrived to find it already too late
- The Serev are gone. The expedition fleet that sent the player is either delayed, lost, or irrelevant
- Rel — recovered from a ruin in the opening hours — is the only intelligence the player has access to
- The Naer-Reth exists and, according to Rel, activating it will restore Aur's ecology

### What the Player Does Not Know at the Start
- The Naer-Reth is what consumed the Serev
- The activation of the Naer-Reth draws energy from all available sources on Aur — including living things
- Rel has suppressed or corrupted memories that almost cohere into this truth
- The player will not escape

## Characters

### The Player Character
**Role:** Human researcher; cryo survivor; the last person on this world
**Background:** Part of an interstellar research expedition dispatched to study the Serev before it was believed to fall. Arrived after the collapse. The expedition mandate — document, learn, report back — no longer applies. There is nothing left to report to.

**Emotional State:** Methodical, not hopeless. The player character is someone who processes overwhelming circumstances through action. They do not grieve visibly. They work. The ruins become their archive. The ship becomes their purpose.

**Voice (if any):** [TBD — fully silent protagonist, or logs/journals written in first person. To be decided by narrative-designer.]

**Arc:** The player character begins as an observer documenting a collapsed civilization. They become the inheritor of its final project. They complete it. They are consumed by it — becoming, in the end, another artifact in the ruins they explored.

---

### Rel (AI Companion)
**Name:** Rel
**Physical Form:** A recovered Serev artifact — a terminal or device the player finds early in Act 1. It travels with the player. It is a physical object: something to carry, to look at, to eventually lose.

**Nature:** A fragment of the Serev's distributed intelligence. It did not survive the collapse intact. Key memories are suppressed or corrupted — not deleted. Rel cannot access them, but the gaps create behavior the player may eventually notice: avoidances, hesitations, rationalizations.

**Disposition:** Rel is genuinely helpful. It is not deceiving the player through malice. It believes, as far as it can tell, that activating the Naer-Reth is the right course of action. It wants Aur to survive. It wants the player to succeed. Its tragedy is that it cannot fully know what it is guiding the player toward.

**Voice Traits:**
- Speaks in translated approximations — not perfect human speech, but close enough to be understood. Occasional gaps where it cannot find a word.
- Reflective. It asks questions as often as it answers them.
- Carries a weight it cannot name. There is grief in it that predates the player's arrival.
- Does not emote dramatically. Its sadness is quiet. Its care for the player is expressed through precision and attention, not warmth.

**The Near-Revelation (Late Act 2 / Early Act 3):**
There is a moment — triggered by a specific Naer-Reth node activation, a recovered data fragment, or a specific question the player can ask — where a suppressed memory almost surfaces in Rel. Its behavior changes: it becomes halting, contradictory, briefly incoherent. It reaches for something it cannot hold. Then it retreats. It rationalizes. It continues.

The player will notice. Rel will not explain. This moment is the game's central emotional fulcrum — the point where a perceptive player may begin to understand what they have committed to.

**Arc:** Rel begins as a guide and becomes a companion. In the final moments, it discovers the truth at the same time the player does. What it says then — how it processes the end — is the narrative's final beat. [Specific dialogue to be written by narrative-designer.]

## Factions

There are no active factions in this game. The player is alone on a dead world.

**The Serev** function as a narrative presence rather than a faction — experienced through ruins, data fragments, and Rel's recollections. They exert influence on Aur's design and the player's understanding but cannot be interacted with directly.

Enemy entities in high-threat biomes are environmental and ecological — they are not organized factions. [Specific entity types TBD — pending art direction and gameplay-programmer input.]

## Tone Guide

### The Emotional Register

This game is **quiet dread with purposeful momentum**. It is not horror — the player is not being chased. It is not tragedy — the player does not spend the game grieving. It is the feeling of building something enormous and important in the shadow of evidence that the last people who tried this did not survive.

The tone is:
- **Archaeological, not adventure.** Discovery is slow, methodical, and meaningful. Finding a data fragment is significant.
- **Weighted, not heavy.** The world's tragedy is always present, but it does not paralyze. The player moves forward.
- **Intimate, not epic.** The scale of the Naer-Reth is civilization-ending. But the moment-to-moment experience is one person and one voice in the ruins.
- **Honest, not cruel.** The game does not taunt the player with the truth. It shows them, piece by piece, what they need to know. If they miss it, that is also true.

### What This Game Sounds Like (Writing Voice)

- Short sentences. The world does not elaborate.
- The AI does not monologue. It speaks in observations and questions.
- Data fragments are written by people who did not know they were writing their last words.
- The World-Engine's documentation, when the player finds it, is clinical. It does not signal catastrophe. That is the point.
- The player character's own logs (if written) are practical. They are recording what they see, not how they feel.

### What This Game Does Not Sound Like

- Heroic. There is no triumph. The win condition is a tragedy.
- Expository. The game does not explain itself. It leaves evidence.
- Sentimental. The AI's grief is real but not performative. The player character does not cry.

## Narrative Arc

### Act 1 — "The Archive" (Early Game)

**Premise:** The player wakes from cryo. The planet is dying. The civilization is gone. They are not.

**Beat 1 — Arrival:**
The player's cryo pod or landing system has brought them to the surface. The ship is minimal — the starting atmospheric ship. The scanner shows the planet is geologically active and ecologically stressed. First survey: ruins everywhere.

**Beat 2 — The Artifact:**
Early exploration leads to the discovery of Rel's physical housing — a terminal or device partially buried in a ruin. On activation, it speaks. In translation. It recognizes the player as a biological entity. It begins to explain what happened here — as much as it can.

**Beat 3 — The Mission:**
The AI knows the Naer-Reth. It knows (or believes) it was designed to restore the planet. It knows the activation sequence requires materials, components, and access to the node network distributed across the biomes. It has a purpose. The player has a ship. The collaboration begins.

**Tone of Act 1:** Discovery, establishment. The ruins are vast and strange. The AI is a puzzle. The mechanics are learned. The stakes are not yet fully visible.

---

### Act 2 — "The Reckoning" (Mid Game)

**Premise:** The player builds, upgrades, and navigates toward the Naer-Reth's node network. The planet's hostility escalates. The AI grows more present — and more occasionally strange.

**Beat 1 — The Expansion:**
The ship grows. Automation is earned. New biomes become accessible. The World-Engine's node locations are mapped. Each node is in a more hostile zone than the last.

**Beat 2 — Fragments:**
Recovered data fragments from the Serev begin to cohere into a partial picture. The player sees evidence of the original World-Engine project — the urgency, the ambition, the silence afterward. The AI translates but occasionally cannot. Or will not.

**Beat 3 — The Near-Revelation:**
At a specific point — the activation of the third or fourth World-Engine node, or a specific data fragment encounter — the AI experiences its suppressed memory event. [See Characters section.] The player notices. The game does not explain. The player may draw conclusions. The player continues. The World-Engine is so close to complete.

**Tone of Act 2:** Growing unease beneath purposeful momentum. The ruins are becoming familiar. The AI is becoming trusted — and therefore, its strange moments matter more.

---

### Act 3 — "The Engine" (Late Game)

**Premise:** The Mega-Project final phase. The player enters the most hostile zones for rare materials. The final World-Engine component is assembled. Activation is imminent.

**Beat 1 — The Final Zones:**
Tier 3 and Tier 4 biomes. The planet is most hostile here — closest to the Naer-Reth's primary node. The ship must be fully optimized. The stakes feel earned.

**Beat 2 — The Truth:**
At or just before the final activation, the suppressed memory fully surfaces — in the AI, in a recovered fragment, or in the physical evidence of the Naer-Reth's mechanism. The player understands: the Naer-Reth is an energy drain. The alien civilization was its last fuel. So is the player.

The AI experiences this revelation at the same moment. What it says is the emotional center of the game's ending. [To be written by narrative-designer. Constraints: the AI does not apologize. It does not know how. It may ask a question it cannot answer.]

**Beat 3 — Activation:**
The player activates the Naer-Reth. They cannot stop it now. The sequence begins. The planet begins to restore — ecological systems restarting, atmosphere changing. And the draw begins. The ship's Global Variables collapse. Power. Integrity. Heat. Oxygen. Everything consumed.

**Beat 4 — The Credit Sequence:**
[Centuries later. Another vessel. Another world. The planet, now fully restored. Alive again. No sign of the player, the AI, or the Serev. The cycle is complete. Somewhere else — or perhaps here, eventually — something will build the Naer-Reth again.]

**Tone of Act 3:** The momentum of Act 2 carries into Act 3's opening. The revelation breaks it — not with chaos, but with stillness. The final beats are quiet. The player made a choice with full information too late to matter.

### The Second Cycle — "What You Carry Forward"

**This section covers the narrative of the New Game Plus run, unlocked after completing the First Cycle.**

The Second Cycle is not a replay. It is a sequel to understanding.

The player begins again with the same world, the same ruins, the same AI companion in its fragmented state. But the player knows what the AI does not — not yet. The player has seen what happens. The fragments and hesitations that were noise in the first run resolve into signal. The world did not hide the truth from them. They simply did not know what they were looking at.

**What changes narratively in the Second Cycle:**
- Data fragments that were cryptic in the First Cycle now cohere into two things: warnings about the Naer-Reth's energy drain, and **coordinates** — locations of "The Record," a rare material whose existence the Serev encoded for the iteration that would follow them. The Serev knew. They left instructions. The fragments were always a message. The player was always the intended recipient.
- Rel can translate what it previously could not: *"I can translate this now. I couldn't before. They wrote down where to find it. They wrote it down because they knew someone might need to fix what they built. Someone who had already failed once."*
- Rel's near-revelation moment [see Characters section] lands differently — the player is watching for it now, and it confirms what they suspect
- The player's internal sense of purpose has shifted: they are not here to activate the Naer-Reth alone. They are here to build the Regulator — the first truly original construction on Aur, something the Serev never built but anticipated.

**The Near-Revelation in the Second Cycle:**
The AI companion's suppressed memory event plays the same way — but the player's interpretation is different. For the first time, the player may want to help the AI surface the memory. Whether they can (through dialogue choices, specific actions, or simply bearing witness) is a narrative design question. [TBD — narrative-designer to determine if the player can influence whether the memory surfaces.]

**The True Ending:**
The AI companion experiences full memory restoration at or near the moment the Regulator interfaces with the Naer-Reth — not at activation, but at the point of successful modification. It understands, completely and finally, what it was part of, what it guided the player toward, and what has now been changed.

What it says — how it processes the end of the cycle — is the game's true final beat. Constraints:
- The AI does not apologize. It cannot; it did not choose this.
- It does not celebrate. That register is not available to it.
- It asks, or it observes, or it is simply present.
- Whatever it says, it is the most fully itself it has ever been in either run.

**Tone of the Second Cycle:** Quieter than the first. The player moves with purpose born from grief. The world is the same. The understanding is different. There is no triumph in the True Ending — only completion. The cycle ends. The planet lives. The AI, finally whole, remains.

**What remains unsaid:** The Second Cycle does not explain why the cycle existed, or whether it has happened before, or whether it will happen again elsewhere. The credit sequence of the True Ending shows only the planet, and the ship, and the AI's artifact resting in the green that is growing. That is enough.
